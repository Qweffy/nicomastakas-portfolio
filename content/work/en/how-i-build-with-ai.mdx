## TL;DR

- I don't use Claude, I operate it. I build and govern the system that produces the answers, the same way I build the machinery that makes features instead of writing them one by one.
- Three layers that almost never live in the same person: real multi-agent orchestration, team-scale configuration and governance, and token-cost discipline (FinOps for LLMs).
- That intersection is exactly what teams are desperate to hire in 2026 and can't find: most "AI engineers" are researchers who can't ship product, or product devs who treat the model as magic.

## The gap

**Most people use the model. Few operate it.**

A good prompt one day, a hallucinated refactor the next, and a bill nobody is watching. Inside a real engineering org that does not hold. The engineer who can put AI to work in a team _and_ keep it from bleeding money is rare, because it takes three skills that seldom overlap. I work at that intersection.

## Layer 1: Real orchestration, not demos

Multi-agent systems doing real, autonomous work, not a toy in a notebook. One coordinates a fleet of agents over an offline-first product; another automates GitHub issues end to end. Several agents working together to resolve actual work on their own. That is my engineering base (monorepos, architecture, offline-first that is not a toy) fused with agent coordination. Few people sustain both at once.

## Layer 2: Configuration and governance at team scale

I configure Claude for an organization, not just my own setup: permissions, governance, standards a whole team inherits. And I shape agent behavior structurally, building system prompts in **Cowork** from the base, not firing off stray prompts to see what sticks. The agent-and-MCP framework I built for a whole engineering team (specialized agents, MCP servers, PR review in the loop) is the same discipline. The practices are public; the proprietary code stays private.

## Layer 3: The token economy (the layer everyone skips)

Anyone can spin up an agent. Almost nobody thinks about the economics of it: burn rate, optimizing the context window, picking the right model per task (Haiku where it is enough, Opus where it is needed), caching, batching. It is FinOps applied to LLMs, and it is the difference between a pretty prototype and something a company can run in production without the invoice bleeding.

> Programming and AI are the same move for me: I do not write the feature, I build the machine that produces features; I do not use the model, I build and govern the system that produces the answers.
>
> *the throughline*

## Always on the frontier

I did not stop at what I learned a year ago. I took the courses, and I stay at the edge: ultracode, Opus 4.8 in VS Code the day it landed. The same self-taught engine that taught me to code keeps me current in a field that moves every week. I run it on my own life too: Claude Code routines that sweep my inbox, workflows for LinkedIn.

## Why it matters

Solid engineering plus AI orchestration plus operational discipline is precisely what teams cannot hire for right now. _I can architect and govern a team's AI tooling, with its economics under control_ is close to a superpower in today's market. For the Staff, Expert, and founding-engineer roles I am after, this is not one more bullet on a CV; it is a pillar of what I bring.
